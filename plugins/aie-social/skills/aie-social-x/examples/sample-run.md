# Sample run — aie-social-x

Condensed transcript of a real run, using the **same research as the LinkedIn sample** (topic: "the four-day workweek") to show how the same source log gets retuned for X. Facts are real; a live run logs full URLs.

## 1. Format decision

Thread. Three distinct supporting points (Microsoft Japan result, UK pilot retention, the meetings mechanism) plus a step the reader can take — too much for 280 characters, right in the 5–9 post sweet spot. **Call: 6-post thread.**

## 2. The thread (delivered)

```
1/ Microsoft Japan tested a 4-day workweek.

Productivity rose about 40%.

The interesting part isn't the number — it's what they cut to get it.

2/ They didn't compress 5 days of work into 4.

They capped meetings at 30 minutes, defaulted to async updates, and made most attendance optional.

The day off was the deadline. The meeting cuts were the mechanism.

3/ The UK ran the bigger test: 61 companies, 6 months.

When the pilot ended, roughly 92% kept the shorter week.

Companies rarely keep policies that cost them money.

4/ Iceland's public-sector trials ran 4 years. Result: shorter hours spread to the majority of the country's workforce.

Three countries, same pattern — output held, hours fell.

5/ The failures share a pattern too: teams that kept every meeting and every process, minus a day.

That's not a shorter week. That's a compressed one.

6/ Want to test it without touching anyone's schedule? Run the meeting audit first: list every recurring meeting, cut or shorten half.

If output holds for a month, you've found your fifth day.

Follow for more on how teams actually change how they work.
```

Every post verified ≤280 characters. No link in the hook; pilot-report link goes in a reply to post 3.
