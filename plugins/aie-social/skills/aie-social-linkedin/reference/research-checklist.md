# Research Checklist (LinkedIn)

Research is what separates this skill from a generic post generator. Do it before you draft. The bar: **every factual claim in the finished post maps to a real, citable source.**

## Step 1 — Define the angle

- What is the **one idea** this post argues? Write it in a single sentence. If you can't, you're not ready to draft.
- Who is the reader, and what do they get from reading? Every post must answer: "How does this help the reader do their job better today?"
- What is the **point of view**? A post with no stance is wallpaper. Pick a defensible angle.

## Step 2 — Gather (web search + fetch)

Run several searches, not one. Vary the phrasing. Look for:

- **Hard numbers** — stats, study results, benchmarks, dates, dollar figures. Specificity builds credibility.
- **Primary sources** — the original study, the company announcement, the documentation, the dataset. Fetch the primary source rather than trusting a blog's paraphrase; summaries drift.
- **Recency** — prefer sources from the last 12-18 months for anything fast-moving. Note the date of every source.
- **Counter-arguments** — what would a smart skeptic say? Addressing the objection makes the post stronger and more honest.
- **Concrete examples** — a named company, a real scenario, a specific before/after. Beats abstraction every time.

If a source document was provided, verify its claims independently and hunt for the sharpest current angle. Do not just compress the input.

## Step 3 — Build the source log

Keep a running list as you go:

```
Claim → Source (URL) → Date → Primary or secondary?
```

Rules:
- Every factual claim needs a row. No row, no claim.
- If two sources disagree, note it and go with the more authoritative/primary one — or say "estimates vary" in the post.
- If you can't source a claim, you have two options: cut it, or reframe it explicitly as opinion ("My take:", "In my experience").
- Never invent a statistic, study, quote, or attribution. "Studies show" with no named study is banned.

## Step 4 — Decide what survives

- Pick the 1-3 facts that best serve the one idea. A LinkedIn post is not a literature review — most of what you gathered stays in the log, not the post.
- Lead with the single most surprising or credible fact; it often becomes the hook.

## Failure modes to avoid

- **Compressing the input instead of researching** — the whole value is fresh facts and a sharp angle.
- **Vague attribution** — "experts agree," "research shows," "it's well known that." Name the source or cut the claim.
- **Stale stats** — a 2019 number presented as current. Check dates.
- **One-source posts** — triangulate anything load-bearing across at least two sources.
- **Overloading** — five stats in a post reads like a slide deck. One or two, well-chosen, wins.
